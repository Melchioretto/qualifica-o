 \begin{otherlanguage*}{english}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------

Wire breakage in enameled copper and aluminum wire factories may result either from defects embedded in the raw material or from degraded equipment conditions. Because operational records do not provide standardized cause labels, the origin of these events cannot be addressed directly as a supervised classification problem. This dissertation proposes WireSense, a hybrid decision-support tool for inferring the origin of wire breakage events by combining sensor-based and historical evidence. The method is developed on data from a factory operating with seventy-seven enameling machines in parallel and is organized into two pillars. The Operational Signals Pillar applies anomaly detection through \emph{Isolation Forest} to sensor time series. The Historical Statistics Pillar analyzes breakage behavior by batch, material, and machine, considering productive exposure in machine-hours and the spread of failures across machines exposed to the same batch. The outputs of both pillars are integrated through deterministic rules represented as decision tables, producing classes of raw-material suspicion, equipment suspicion, parallel investigation, or insufficient evidence. The proposal prioritizes interpretability and auditability in an industrial scenario without reliable cause labels. The systematic literature review identified sixteen related works and highlighted the lack of solutions that integrate anomaly detection, historical analysis, and interpretable evidence fusion in the domain of enameled wire manufacturing. Retrospective validation over events with documented causes and deployment under a continuous inference regime constitute the work planned up to the defense.

   \textbf{Keywords}: wire breakage. anomaly detection. historical statistics. evidence fusion. industrial decision support.
 \end{otherlanguage*}
\end{resumo}
